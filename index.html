How Plays Work

Each play is built around a trigger -- a signal that something is changing in a customer's business, team, or strategy. When you spot a trigger, run the play.

OpportunityWhat you can gain by acting: expansion, retention, advocacy, deeper relationship.
RiskWhat you lose by not acting: churn, revenue loss, competitor foothold.

Customer Health = Green

Growth & Advocacy

Trigger
Customer health score is Green. Account is stable, engaged, and satisfied.

Opportunity

  • Reinforce value and satisfaction before complacency sets in
  • Explore upsell or expansion opportunities (new countries, products)
  • Capture advocacy: testimonials, case studies, referrals

Risk

  • Green does not mean "forever" -- value may be fragile if not actively reinforced
  • Quiet champions may change roles without notice (see POC Change play)

Actions

  2. Explore what's next -- new markets, new features, deeper adoption. Ask about their roadmap.
  3. Ask for advocacy -- testimonials, case studies, or referrals if timing is right
  4. Keep them informed -- share relevant country/market insights, new product updates
  5. Schedule QBR or monthly check-in to maintain cadence

G2

Expansion Signals

EEs with other vendors / consolidation opportunity

Trigger
Customer has EEs managed by another vendor, or you learn they're using multiple EOR providers across regions.

Opportunity

  • Consolidate EEs under one vendor for simplification of global workforce management
  • Reduce client admin overhead and improve consistency
  • Increase revenue per account

Risk

  • Lose EEs to competitor if they consolidate away from us
  • Client may not see the value of switching providers for existing EEs

Actions

  1. Inquire about their full global hiring setup -- don't assume you know all vendors involved
  2. Ask about contractors -- are they part of the mix? Misclassification risk is a lever
  3. Position consolidation as simplification -- one partner, one invoice, one compliance layer

Messaging Template

I'd love to get a better sense of your global hiring setup. Are you currently working with multiple vendors or handling things region by region?

Are contractors part of your mix, or are you mostly focused on full-time employees?

It could be worth exploring whether there's an opportunity to simplify things under one partner to reduce admin and improve consistency. Would you be open to that?
Channel: Check-in / Monthly / QBR Call

R6

Collections (Past Due Invoices)

Your role, your one touchpoint, and exactly what to say

Trigger
AR flags you in Slack around Day 7 past due: "No response from [Client]. Can you do a quick check-in?" This is your one touchpoint.

How Collections Works

AR/Collections owns the entire collections cadence. They send the reminders, track the status, enforce late fees, escalate, and trigger offboarding. That is their job.

Your role is one specific moment: when AR has sent two reminders and gotten no response (around day 7 past due), they will flag you in Slack and ask you to do a quick personal check-in with the client. That is it. One touchpoint, then AR takes it back.

Why Your One Touch Matters
You have a relationship with this client. A quick personal message from you will get a response faster than a fifth AR email ever will. You are not chasing money. You are opening a door that AR cannot open on their own.

The Process: Step by Step

WhenWhat HappensYour Role
Day -5Upflow sends automated reminder to clientNone. You are copied for awareness.
Day -1Upflow sends second reminder with payment linkNone. You are copied.
Day +3Upflow alerts AR team that invoice is past dueNone. AR begins their outreach.
Day +7AR flags you in Slack: "No response from [Client]. Can you do a quick check-in?"YOUR ONE TOUCH. See messaging below.
Day +8You report back to AR what you learnedQuick Slack reply with the outcome.
Day 8 to 60AR handles all follow-ups, late fees, escalationNone. AR owns the cadence.
Day 60+Offboarding triggered if unresolved (VP Ops approves)You are informed. You do not execute.

Your One Touch: What to Say

When AR flags you, reach out personally and find out what is going on. Use whatever channel is most natural for that client: email, WhatsApp, a phone call, or all three.

Option A: Email

Subject: Quick heads up on your account

Hi [First Name],

I wanted to reach out personally. I noticed there is an outstanding invoice on your account ([#INV-XXXX] for [amount], due [date]), and I want to make sure nothing on our end is causing a hold-up.

Is there anything going on I should know about? A PO issue, a question on the invoice, a change in your AP process? Happy to help sort it out if so.

Just wanted to flag it before it turns into a bigger thing for either of us.

Thanks,
[Your Name]
Channel: Email

Option B: WhatsApp or Text

Hey [First Name], hope you are well. Quick heads up -- there is an invoice on your account that is a bit overdue and I wanted to flag it before it gets escalated. Anything holding it up on your end? Happy to help if so.
Channel: WhatsApp / Text

Option C: Phone Call

Sandler Approach: Pain Discovery
"Hey [Name], I noticed an invoice came up as overdue on your account. I am sure there is a reason for it. Help me understand what is going on so I can figure out if there is something I need to fix on my end."

This positions you as a partner, not a collector. It gives the client permission to share what is really happening. Listen, take notes, and relay what you learn to AR.

Reporting Back to AR

After your touchpoint, reply to the AR Slack flag within 24 hours. Keep it simple. AR just needs three things:

Tell ARExample
Did they respond?"Spoke with Jane. She responded same day."
What is the blocker?"AP team changed. New contact is finance@acme.com. Invoice needs to be re-sent."
Any commitment?"She said payment will be processed by March 15. No disputes."

If you got no response: that is useful information too. Tell AR: "Tried email and WhatsApp, no response. Recommend AR continues the cadence." Then you are done.

Boundaries: What Is and Is Not Your Job

Your JobNot Your Job
One personal check-in when AR flags youSending multiple follow-up emails about payment
Finding out what is blocking paymentNegotiating payment plans or late fee waivers
Reporting back to AR within 24 hoursTracking the account through the full cadence
Being a bridge between the client and ARSending contract breach or escalation warnings
Providing context if offboarding is triggeredContacting employees or discussing severance
Staying professional if the client reaches out to youPromising to reverse or delay the collections process

If an Account Reaches Offboarding

If a client is unresponsive after the full AR cadence, the Collections team may trigger offboarding (approved by VP Ops). You will be informed in #collections-offboarding. You do not execute any part of the offboarding process.

One Exception
Juan C. may ask you to send the final client notice before offboarding begins. If that happens, he will give you the specific instructions and copy. Do not send anything on your own.

If the Client Reaches Out During Offboarding

Acknowledge their message and route them to the right team:

Thank you for reaching out. The process has moved to our collections team at this point. Let me connect you with the right people to see what options are available.
The Bottom Line
You are not a collector. You are the person with the relationship. AR needs you for one thing: opening a door they cannot open. Do that well, report back, and let them handle the rest. Your time is better spent on renewals, expansions, and keeping your healthy clients happy.

Training Philosophy

The principles behind how we build CSMs at Remote People

Core Beliefs

1. CSMs are commercial, not just relational

Every CSM at Remote People is expected to identify expansion opportunities, protect revenue, and drive commercial outcomes. We don't separate "relationship management" from "revenue management" because they're the same thing done well. Training reflects this from day one.

2. EOR knowledge is a competitive advantage

Any CSM can learn Sandler methodology or HubSpot workflows. What makes our team different is deep understanding of the EOR industry: compliance, entity economics, country-specific nuances, and competitive positioning. We invest disproportionately in EOR-specific training.

3. Practice beats theory

We don't do death-by-PowerPoint training. Every session includes hands-on practice: role plays, live call reviews, scenario walkthroughs, or real account analysis. If someone can't apply it to a real account the same week, the training failed.

4. The team teaches the team

Senior CSMs lead sessions, share war stories, and mentor new hires. This scales knowledge, builds leadership skills, and keeps training grounded in reality rather than theory.

5. Feedback is immediate and specific

We don't wait for quarterly reviews to tell someone how they're doing. Call reviews happen weekly. Feedback is specific, actionable, and delivered with care. We use QA scorecards so feedback is objective, not personal.

The Sandler Foundation

Our client conversations are rooted in Sandler methodology. New hires don't need prior Sandler certification, but they will learn and practice these core concepts during ramp:

Milestones & Checkpoints

Clear gates that a new CSM must pass before advancing

W2

End of Week 2: Foundation Complete

Pass written assessment (80%+). Can explain EOR model, name all products, describe 5 competitors, and navigate HubSpot.

W4

End of Week 4: Shadowing Complete

12+ calls documented in Shadowing Log. Completed role play exercises. Buddy confirms readiness for guided practice.

W6

End of Week 6: First Calls Solo

Run 4+ client calls without buddy on the call. Up-front contract used on every call. QA scores: 65+ average.

W8

End of Week 8: Mid-Ramp Assessment

Managing 5 to 8 accounts. Mock QBR delivered. 1+ playbook executed end-to-end. QA scores: 70+ average. Head of CS sign-off for Phase 4.

W12

End of Week 12: Certification

Full book managed independently. Written assessment passed (80%+). Live role play passed. Account review presented. QA scores: 80+ average. Officially certified.

If a Milestone Is Missed
Missing a milestone doesn't mean failure. It means more support is needed. The Head of CS and buddy will create a 2-week remediation plan targeting the specific gap. The CSM repeats the assessment after the remediation period. Two consecutive missed milestones require a performance conversation.

Session Rules

  • Camera on. Training is interactive. If you can't be present, watch the recording and submit written reflections.
  • No spectators. Every session requires participation: role play, share a perspective, ask a question, present something.
  • Record everything. All sessions recorded and stored in Google Drive for async access across time zones.
  • Rotate leaders. Week 2 and Week 4 sessions are led by different CSMs each month. This builds leadership skills and keeps content fresh.
  • Action items. Every session ends with 1 to 2 takeaways each person commits to applying that week.

Buddy Selection Criteria

Not every senior CSM is a good buddy. Select based on:

  • Patience: willing to answer the same question multiple times without frustration
  • Call quality: consistently scores 85+ on QA scorecard (they model the right behaviors)
  • Availability: has time in their schedule (not managing 20+ Tier 1 accounts at the same time)
  • Communication: gives specific, constructive feedback, not just "that was good"
  • Enthusiasm: genuinely wants to help someone grow (this can't be forced)
Time Investment
Being a buddy takes approximately 5 to 8 hours per week during Weeks 1 to 4, dropping to 2 to 3 hours per week during Weeks 5 to 8. Factor this into the buddy's account load and expectations. Recognize and reward great buddies -- it's one of the most impactful things a CSM can do for the team.

QA & Assessment Framework

How we measure and improve call quality across the team

QA Scorecard (Detailed)

Each dimension is scored 1 to 5. Total possible score: 35. Percentage calculated against total. Target for ramped CSMs: 80%+ (28+/35).

Dimension1 (Needs Work)3 (Meets Standard)5 (Exceptional)
Call Opening No agenda set. Jumped straight into content. No time check. Up-front contract used. Agenda stated. Time confirmed. Up-front contract feels natural. Permission to be direct granted. Client co-owns the agenda.
Discovery Mostly closed questions. CSM talked more than client. Surface-level. Open questions used. Client shared meaningful information. Some follow-up depth. Pain funnel deployed. Multiple layers of "tell me more." Client shared something they hadn't planned to.
EOR Knowledge Inaccurate information shared. Couldn't answer basic compliance questions. Accurate product and compliance information. Handled questions confidently. Proactively shared country-specific insights. Connected compliance to business impact. Client learned something new.
Commercial Awareness No expansion signals identified. No products mentioned. Purely relational call. Identified at least 1 signal. Raised a product or service naturally. Connected client's business goals to specific expansion opportunity. Created urgency without pressure.
Objection Handling Got defensive. Caved immediately. Made promises without authority. Stayed calm. Used reversing. Addressed the concern without over-committing. Turned the objection into a deeper conversation. Client felt heard AND moved forward.
Call Close Call ended without clear next steps. "Let's reconnect sometime." Next steps stated. Follow-up date set. Action items assigned. Mutual commitments confirmed. Client and CSM both have deliverables. Energy and trust increased.
Post-Call No HubSpot log. No follow-up email. Notes are vague or missing. HubSpot logged within 24h. Follow-up email sent. Notes are clear and actionable. Detailed notes with next steps, signals, and sentiment captured. Follow-up email is personalized and value-adding.
